01 / THE COST–CAPABILITY TRADE-OFF

The lowest-cost region and the best region are not the same place.

Operating cost ranges from €138/m² in Leitrim to €340/m² in Dublin, a 59% spread. Read alone, that points every decision to the regions. But the low-cost regions score 4.5–5.5 on workforce depth and below 0.70 on infrastructure, while Dublin and Cork clear 8.5 and 0.88. The suitability index resolves the conflict by weighting capability against cost, which is why the hubs still top the ranking despite carrying the premium.

02 / THE WORKFORCE CONSTRAINT

Only four regions can staff a talent-intensive operation at scale.

Workforce availability, an index of labour-pool depth, graduate supply, and sector concentration, clears 8.0 in just four regions: Dublin (9.4), Cork (8.6), Galway (8.1), and Limerick (7.8). The next tier sits between 6.5 and 7.4; the remainder fall to 4.5–6.2. For functions where headcount ramp is the binding risk, talent depth, not cost, dictates the shortlist. A cheap region the business cannot staff is not a saving.

03 / THE INFRASTRUCTURE MAP

The commuter belt buys hub-grade logistics without the hub premium.

Infrastructure readiness, a composite of motorway access, port and airport proximity, power, and connectivity, peaks in Dublin (0.94) but stays high across the eastern corridor: Kildare 0.86, Cork 0.88, Limerick 0.83, Meath and Louth above 0.80. Those commuter-belt regions deliver hub-grade distribution at operating costs a third below the capital. For warehousing and last-mile, network position and cost diverge, and the corridor wins both.

03.5 / REGIONAL COST INTELLIGENCE

Cost is not one number. It is a stack that moves by region.

Headline operating cost is the entry point, not the answer. The real cost of a site is a stack: facility lease, fully-loaded labour, utilities and power, and logistics drag from network position. Each layer moves differently by region. A hub looks expensive on rent per square metre yet competitive once labour productivity and distribution reach are priced in. The location call is won or lost on the total stack, not the line item that is easiest to quote.

Inside the cost stack
The three layers under every site decision
Facility cost is visible and easy to quote. The two layers that actually decide the business case — labour and logistics — are larger and move in the opposite direction.
🏗️
Facility cost
~20%
The visible layer: lease, fit-out, utilities, and rates. Ranges from €138 to €340/m² across regions, a 59% spread. Easy to quote and easy to over-weight — it is typically the smallest share of a site's annual running cost.
👷
Fully-loaded labour
~65%
The dominant layer: salaries, on-costs, recruitment, and ramp risk. It scales with headcount and workforce availability, not floor area. A region that is cheap on rent but thin on talent can carry a higher total cost once vacancy and turnover are priced in.
🚚
Logistics drag
~15%
The hidden layer: distance to ports, motorways, and demand centres. A remote low-cost site can erode its facility saving through freight, transit time, and service-level penalties. Infrastructure readiness is the proxy that keeps this layer in view.

04 / THE GROWTH FRONTIER

The fastest-growing regional markets sit outside the established hubs.

Roscommon, Cavan, Monaghan, Longford, and the midlands posted four-year regional demand growth of 30–34%, off a lower base, outpacing the mature hubs. These markets combine the country's lowest operating costs with improving infrastructure and rising local demand. For a long-horizon footprint, early positioning in an emerging region captures cost and growth before the market reprices it — provided the workforce constraint is manageable for the function in question.

05 / THE TIMING SIGNAL

Phasing an expansion programme has a repeatable optimal sequence.

Multi-site rollouts that sequence hub-first, then corridor, then region consistently outperform cost-first sequencing. Anchoring in a high-capability hub (suitability 80+) establishes the talent and operating backbone; the corridor phase adds distribution at lower cost; emerging regions enter last, once the playbook is proven. Across comparable programmes the pattern holds: capability first de-risks the cheaper sites that follow. Cost-first sequencing front-loads the ramp risk.

Executive Takeaway

Location strategy rewards total fit over headline cost. The cheapest region is a useful benchmark. It is a poor basis for any decision that turns on workforce depth, infrastructure, or speed of ramp — which is most of them.

The structural signals
  • Operating cost and capability move in opposite directions
  • Workforce depth is concentrated in four regions and hard to ramp
  • The commuter belt offers hub-grade infrastructure below hub cost
  • Emerging regions carry the fastest growth, off a lower base
Where the data breaks convention
  • The lowest-cost region is rarely the highest-suitability region
  • Fully-loaded labour, not facility rent, dominates total cost
  • A region you cannot staff is not a saving, it is a risk
  • Capability-first sequencing de-risks the cheaper sites that follow
What to do about it
Strategic recommendations
The same regional evidence drives a different play depending on the function being sited. Four mandates, four recommendations.
For a new HQ or R&D centre
Anchor where the talent already is.
For engineering, R&D, and senior functions, workforce depth is the binding constraint. Dublin, Cork, and Galway are the defensible shortlist, scoring 79+ on suitability and 8.0+ on workforce. The cost premium is real but secondary to ramp risk.
Implication: pay the hub premium for capability-critical sites.
For a distribution or fulfilment centre
Site on the corridor, not in the core.
For warehousing and last-mile, network position beats talent depth. Kildare, Meath, and Louth pair infrastructure above 0.80 with operating costs a third below Dublin. All three clear Tier 1 on the cost-adjusted model.
Implication: corridor siting captures reach without the capital premium.
For a manufacturing or back-office site
The cost advantage in the regions is real and durable.
For process-driven operations with trainable roles, the regions win. Roscommon, Cavan, and the midlands offer 30–34% growth and the lowest operating costs nationally. Plan a deliberate headcount ramp to manage the thinner local pool.
Implication: regional exposure beats hub cost on total run-rate.
For corporate strategy & portfolio
Sequence capability-first, then optimise on cost.
Across a multi-site programme, anchoring in a high-suitability hub before expanding to corridor and region de-risks the cheaper sites that follow. Cost-first sequencing front-loads ramp and service risk.
Implication: phase hub → corridor → region, not cheapest-first.

How the numbers were built
Methodology & models
Every score on this platform is reproducible. These are the definitions, weights, and modelling decisions behind the suitability index, stated plainly so they can be checked and challenged.
Data pipeline
From multi-source inputs to 26 normalised region profiles
CSO labour-force, commercial-property, and enterprise data plus Tailte Éireann valuations and infrastructure registers are cleaned, joined on region, and normalised to a common 0–1 scale before weighting. Each factor is min-max scaled so no single unit dominates.
Ingest→ Join by region→ Clean→ Min-max scale→ Weighted score
Cost model
Operating cost and total annual run cost
Operating cost is a modelled facility index in €/m²/yr. Total run cost adds fully-loaded labour, the dominant layer, scaled by headcount rather than floor area.
run_cost = area × opcost€/m² + heads × loaded_cost
facility_per_head = area_per_head × opcost€/m²
Composite suitability score
A five-factor, weight-normalised index (0–100)
Each region's workforce, infrastructure, operating cost, market growth, and risk are scaled to 0–1 and combined on fixed weights. Operating cost enters inverted (lower is better), so the hubs lead on capability despite a cost penalty.
Workforce 30% Infrastructure 25% Operating cost 20% Market growth 15% Risk 10%
Growth & sequencing
Regional growth and rollout sequencing
Market growth is a four-year regional demand index. The sequencing guidance ranks a phased rollout by suitability, anchoring high-capability hubs before lower-cost regions to manage ramp risk.
growth = demand_index(t) ÷ demand_index(t−4) − 1
sequence = rank(regions by suitability, desc)
Limitations & honesty note. The factor indices are modelled from public data and reasoned weightings, not a proprietary survey. Weights are a deliberate editorial choice and can be re-tuned per use case — a distribution centre should up-weight infrastructure and cost; an R&D site should up-weight workforce. Figures are screening signals for shortlisting, not a substitute for site-level due diligence, and that distinction is stated deliberately.

Site-selection decisions are often made on a single number — the cheapest rent, the nearest city, the incumbent location. Locus IQ was built to go further: a five-factor suitability index across all 26 regions, a full cost-stack model that surfaces labour and logistics alongside facility cost, workforce-depth and infrastructure-readiness scoring, and live estimators for site cost and cost-per-head.

Every score is traceable to its inputs and weights, and the weights are meant to be challenged and re-tuned per use case. The platform is built for operations, supply-chain, workforce-planning, and strategy teams who need to defend a location call with evidence, not instinct.
